At the Shoe Shine Stand

2010, 2:42 min, color, sound, HD video

“At the Shoe Shine Stand” (2010) by Jayson Musson is a short video artwork that blends humor with sharp social commentary. In the piece, a white man walks up to a traditional shoe shine stand expecting to have his shoes polished, but instead, his face is buffed and polished with a wax that matches the skin tones of famous Black celebrities like Oprah Winfrey, Eddie Murphy, and Barack Obama. Presented as a comedic skit, the video cleverly flips racial dynamics and exposes how Black identity is often consumed, imitated, and commodified within American culture. While the action seems absurd at first, it forces viewers to think about the history of racial stereotypes, the lingering imagery of minstrel shows, and the ways pop culture can distort or profit from Blackness. Musson uses this simple yet unsettling exchange to highlight the complexities of representation, privilege, and appropriation in a post-Obama era, where the visibility of Black figures in media does not necessarily equal equality or understanding.
